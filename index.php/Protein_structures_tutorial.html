"Get me started" mini task[edit]

This task is designed to help you become familiar with protein structures, RMSDs, and Pymol.

1) Download the experimental protein structure for ubiquitin, which has the PDB code 1UBQ. You can get this at www.pdb.org. You want the simple pdb text file.

2) Copy the file over to your home folder (or a subfolder) on the group cluster.

3) Now we want to energy-minimize the structure so as to relax it to a state in which the force on each atom is zero. Here, by energy-minimize, we mean the potential energy, which is due to interactions between the protein atoms (given by a force field that is represented analytically in the computer). To energy minimize, use the pdbtools program. You can execute the command "pdbtools.py minimize file.pdb --suffix=_min" where file.pdb is what you downloaded. This will produce file_min.pdb, which is a minimized version of the original. This may take a short amount of time, actually. Subsequently, run a more thorough minimization using the command "pdbtools.py longminimize file.pdb --suffix=_min2" to produce file_min2.pdb. This will take a longer time, but no longer than 10 min.

4) Compare the potential energies of the different structures to assess the impact of the minimization. To return the energies, use "pdbtools.py getenergies file*.pdb".

5) We also want to measure how different the structures are. To do this, we measure the root-mean-squared deviation of backbone atoms in the protein (entailing a sum over squared distances between the positions of the atoms in the two structures), or the RMSD for short. Before computing the RMSD between two protein structures, however, one needs to align the structures so that they produce the minimum RMSD possible. We have automated ways of doing this in our lab. To compute the RMSDs between the different proteins, run "rmsd.py file*.pdb". The numbers produced here are given in Angstrom (note that 1 A is about the radius of a hydrogen atom.) Consider how different the structures are, in terms of RMSD. Generally < 3A RMSD means structures are very similar, with only small differences noticeable. Some links about RMSD you may want to read are:

  • http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protein_structural_alignment
  • http://boscoh.com/protein/rmsd-root-mean-square-deviation

6) Copy the original structure and minimized files back to your local workstation computer.

7) Open Pymol. Open in it the three files, in sequence. You will see these appear in the list to the right. First, align all of the structures to the first one. Click on the "A" button next to the first file, then > Align > all to this. Then you need to click on the Reset button at the screen top. Second, change the representation to a "Cartoon" view. Under the "S" button next to "all", click Show as > Cartoon. Then, examine where in the structures there are differences. What does energy minimization improve here? Note that you can turn structures on or off by clicking their names.

8) You can then play around with Pymol to explore its different commands. There is an excellent tutorial at http://www.ebi.ac.uk/~gareth/pymol/pymol.shtml.
